# ACL Rehabilitation: Evidence-Based Best Practices for Reduced Re-Tear Rate

**From Prehab Through Return to Sport**

*Research compiled March 2026. This document synthesizes findings from peer-reviewed systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and landmark cohort studies. It is not medical advice — always consult your surgeon and physical therapist.*

---

## Quantitative Phase Transition Criteria (Study-Defined Gates)

> **Important caveat:** The research literature does not consistently define gate criteria between every phase. What follows are the specific quantitative thresholds that have been explicitly stated in published studies or clinical commentaries. Where criteria are sparse, that is noted honestly. Criteria are organized by transition point, not by phase name.

---

### Gate 1 → Clearing Phase 1 (Post-Op): The "Quiet Knee" Criteria

**Source:** Buckthorpe et al. (2020) — *"A Ten Task-Based Progression in Rehabilitation After ACL Reconstruction"*, International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy; reinforced by general criterion-based literature.

Before progressing past the immediate post-operative phase and discontinuing crutches, the following must all be met:

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Active knee extension | Full (0°) — no extension deficit |
| Quadriceps lag on SLR | None — must perform SLR without lag |
| Effusion (swelling) | Trace or zero — no increase >1 cm at the patella after exercise |
| Pain during exercises | 0–2/10 on visual analogue scale (VAS) |
| Knee flexion ROM | Progressing toward 90° (goal by end of week 2) |

> **Note:** The brace/immobilizer is discontinued only once the patient can perform an SLR without an extension lag. This is the most consistently defined early gate in the literature.

---

### Gate 2 → Entering Progressive Strengthening (Phase 2→3): Intermediate Milestones

**Source:** Criterion-based rehabilitation literature (Wilk et al., 2012 — *JOSPT*; Buckthorpe et al., 2020).

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Knee flexion ROM | Within 10° of the uninvolved side (or ≥120°) |
| Quadriceps index (isokinetic or isometric LSI) | >60% — minimum to enter progressive strengthening |
| Effusion | Trace or zero at rest and after exercise |
| Gait | Normal pattern, no assistive device |
| Pain | None with daily activities |

> **Note:** The 60% quad LSI threshold is cited as the floor for entering the progressive strengthening phase. It is not a clearance threshold for running or sport — it is the minimum to start loading more seriously.

---

### Gate 3 → Return to Running: The Most Evidence-Defined Intermediate Gate

**Source:** Rambaud et al. (2018) — *"Criteria for Return to Running After ACL Reconstruction: A Scoping Review"*, BJSM. Reviewed 201 studies. Also supported by clinical data review (Grindem group).

This is the best-evidenced intermediate gate. The most frequently reported criteria across studies were:

| Criterion | Threshold | Evidence Level |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Time post-surgery | ≥8–12 weeks (median 12 weeks across 201 studies) | Strong — Rambaud 2018 |
| Knee flexion ROM | Full, or ≥95% of uninvolved side | Most common in literature |
| Pain during running | None, or <2/10 VAS | Most common in literature |
| Isometric quad LSI | ≥70% | Most common threshold — Rambaud 2018 |
| Isometric hamstring LSI | ≥70% | Most common threshold — Rambaud 2018 |
| Hop test LSI | ≥70% | Most common threshold — Rambaud 2018 |
| Effusion | Trace or zero | Standard clinical requirement |

> **Critical finding:** Rambaud et al. (2018) found that fewer than 1 in 5 studies even reported clinical or strength-based criteria for return to running — most relied on time alone. The thresholds above represent the best-available evidence, not universal consensus.

> One specific protocol (referenced in the ACL clinical data review) defined running clearance as: ≥8 weeks post-surgery + quad index ≥80% + trace or less effusion + patient demonstrates understanding of the "soreness rules."

---

### Gate 4 → Initiating Plyometrics: The Buckthorpe 4-Stage Model

**Source:** Buckthorpe & Villa (2021) — *"Recommendations for Plyometric Training After ACL Reconstruction: A Clinical Commentary"*, International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy. This is the most detailed published framework for plyometric progression with explicit quantitative gate criteria.

The program has 4 stages. Athletes cannot enter a stage without meeting all criteria.

#### Stage 1 Entry — Bilateral Plyometrics (approx. months 3–4)

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Knee flexion ROM | ≥120° |
| Bilateral squat loading asymmetry | <20% between limbs |
| Isometric quad LSI | >70% of uninvolved side |
| Pain | None during bilateral loading |
| Effusion | Trace or zero |

#### Stage 2 Entry — Single-Leg Landing and Jumping (approx. months 4–5)

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Single-leg squat quality | Good control — no excessive dynamic knee valgus, no altered trunk/pelvic strategy |
| Single-leg leg press load | >1.25× body mass |
| Quad LSI (knee extensor) | >80% |
| Treadmill running | Able to run with good kinematics |

#### Stage 3 Entry — Advanced and Reactive Single-Leg Plyometrics (approx. months 5–6)

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Quad LSI | >80% |
| Single-leg landing mechanics | Good quality under fatigue, no valgus collapse |
| Pain/effusion | None after stage 2 plyometric sessions |

#### Stage 4 Entry — Sport-Specific and High-Intensity Plyometrics (approx. months 6+)

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| Quad LSI | Approaching 90% |
| Single-leg performance | All stage 3 tasks performed with good mechanics |
| Cleared to advance toward return-to-sport testing |  |

> **Summary of Buckthorpe's LSI progression ladder:**
> - LSI ≥70% → cleared to jog on treadmill and begin bilateral plyometrics
> - LSI ≥80% → cleared for single-leg landing, jumping drills, and advanced plyometrics
> - LSI ≥90% → cleared for return-to-sport training and testing

---

### Gate 5 → Return to Full Sport: The Highest-Evidence Gate

**Source:** Grindem et al. (2016) — Delaware-Oslo ACL Cohort; Kyritsis et al. (2016); van Melick et al. (2016) consensus statement.

This is the most rigorously studied gate, with the clearest quantitative thresholds.

| Criterion | Threshold | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Time post-surgery | ≥9 months | Grindem 2016 — 51% lower risk per month delayed |
| Isokinetic quad LSI (60°/s) | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016; Grindem 2016 |
| Isokinetic quad LSI (180°/s) | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016 |
| Isokinetic hamstring LSI | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016 |
| Single hop for distance LSI | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016 |
| Triple hop for distance LSI | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016 |
| Triple crossover hop LSI | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016 |
| 6-m timed hop LSI | ≥90% | Kyritsis 2016 |
| Running T-test | ≤11 seconds | Kyritsis 2016 |
| Effusion | Trace or zero | Clinical standard |
| ROM | Full, pain-free | Clinical standard |
| ACL-RSI (psychological readiness) | ≥80% | Ardern et al. 2014 |
| KOOS Sport/Recreation subscale | ≥90% | Delaware-Oslo consensus |

> **Key finding from Grindem 2016:** For every 1% decrease in quad strength below 90% LSI at return to sport, re-injury risk increases by approximately 3%. This is the strongest dose-response relationship in the entire ACL rehabilitation literature.

> **Key finding from Kyritsis 2016:** Athletes who did NOT meet all 6 discharge criteria had a hazard ratio of 4.1 for graft rupture — a 4× greater risk. This is the closest thing to a validated RTS gate battery in the published literature.

---

### What the Literature Does NOT Define Well

To be transparent: the research is most specific at the two ends (early post-op and return to sport). Intermediate gates — particularly the transition from Phase 2 to Phase 3 strengthening, and the clearance to begin sport-specific agility work — are poorly standardized. A 2025 scoping review in *JOSPT Open* found that only 5% of published ACL rehabilitation programs reported reproducible, criterion-based presurgery or early postsurgery protocols. This is an acknowledged gap in the field.

---

## Executive Summary

ACL graft failure occurs in roughly 5–25% of patients, and revision surgery means another 9–12 months of recovery. The research is clear: how you rehabilitate matters as much as the surgery itself. Two landmark findings frame every phase of this guide:

1. **Delaying return to sport until at least 9 months** reduces reinjury risk by 51% for each month delayed up to that point (Grindem et al., 2016 — Delaware-Oslo ACL Cohort Study).
2. **Passing a battery of functional return-to-sport tests** before returning is associated with a 4x lower graft rupture risk compared to those who fail testing (Kyritsis et al., 2016).

This report walks through every phase — from the moment of injury through full return to competition — with specific exercises, do's, don'ts, and the studies that back them up.

## Phase 0: Prehabilitation (Injury → Surgery Day)

### Why It Matters

Prehabilitation is one of the most underutilized tools in ACL recovery. A 2025 systematic review by **Zakharia et al.** in *Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology, Arthroscopy* examined 36 studies with 2,326 patients and found that prehab produced zero preoperative complications and that clinical outcomes met or surpassed Patient Acceptable Symptom State (PASS) thresholds — with functional improvements maintained up to 10 years post-operation.

A 2020 systematic review by **Niederer et al.** in *PLOS ONE* found that prehab groups achieved significantly higher self-reported knee function both before surgery and two years after, and that return-to-sport rates were higher at two years.

The 2024 meta-analysis by **Aryana et al.** confirmed significantly higher 2-year KOOS scores across all subscales (pain, symptoms, ADL, sport/recreation, quality of life; all p < 0.0001) in prehab groups.

### Duration and Structure

Research supports **4–6 weeks** of structured prehabilitation, **2–4 sessions per week**, for 75–120 minutes per session (Cunha & Solomon, 2022). The target is to enter surgery in the best possible condition.

### Goals Before Surgery

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Quadriceps strength (vs. uninjured leg) | ≥80%, ideally ≥90% |
| Hamstring strength (vs. uninjured leg) | ≥90% |
| Range of motion | Full extension (0°), flexion to at least 120° |
| Gait | Normal, no limp |
| Swelling | Minimal to none |
| Single-leg hop performance | ≥90% of uninjured leg |

### Key Exercises — Prehab Phase

**Quadriceps Activation and Strengthening**
- Quad sets (isometric contractions, 10-second holds)
- Straight leg raises (SLR) in all four directions
- Mini-squats and wall sits (pain-free range)
- Leg press (closed kinetic chain)
- Step-ups and step-downs

**Range of Motion**
- Heel slides
- Prone knee flexion
- Prone hangs for terminal extension

**Neuromuscular and Proprioceptive Training**
- Single-leg stance (eyes open progressing to eyes closed)
- Balance board and wobble board work
- Perturbation training
- Weight shifts (forward, backward, lateral)

**Hamstring and Hip Strengthening**
- Hamstring curls (prone)
- Glute bridges progressing to single-leg bridges
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Clamshells with resistance band

### Do's and Don'ts — Prehab

| DO | DON'T |
|-----|-------|
| Prioritize full knee extension — it's harder to regain after surgery | Ignore swelling — reduce it before progressing exercises |
| Train the uninjured leg too (cross-education effect) | Push through sharp pain or instability episodes |
| Work on proprioception and balance daily | Perform cutting, pivoting, or high-impact activities |
| Achieve quad strength ≥80% before surgery | Rush to surgery before regaining baseline function |
| Address any gait abnormalities | Neglect hamstrings and hip musculature |

## Phase 1: Immediate Post-Operative (Weeks 0–2)

### What the Research Says

The first two weeks are about protecting the graft while combating the rapid onset of quadriceps inhibition (arthrogenic muscle inhibition). **Quadriceps strength deficits of 15–40%** are typical after ACL injury and surgery, and preoperative quad strength is a significant predictor of long-term knee function (Adams et al., 2012).

### Goals

- Protect the healing graft
- Achieve and maintain **full knee extension (0°)** — this is the single most critical early goal
- Reduce pain and swelling
- Restore patellar mobility
- Begin quad activation
- Achieve independent straight leg raise without extension lag

### Key Exercises — Phase 1

- **Quad sets**: Isometric contractions, 10-second holds, performed frequently throughout the day (every waking hour if possible)
- **Ankle pumps / calf pumps**: For circulation and DVT prevention
- **Heel slides / wall slides**: Gentle flexion ROM work (target 90° by end of week 2)
- **Patellar mobilizations**: Superior, inferior, medial, lateral glides
- **Prone hangs**: Passive terminal knee extension — let gravity do the work
- **Straight leg raises**: Begin once quad set is solid, progress through all four hip directions
- **Hamstring isometrics**: Gentle activation
- **Weight shifting**: In standing with crutch support

### Do's and Don'ts — Phase 1

| DO | DON'T |
|-----|-------|
| Ice and elevate aggressively to manage swelling | Force flexion — let it come gradually |
| Prioritize full extension above all else | Bear full weight before cleared by surgeon |
| Perform quad sets 100+ times per day | Ignore signs of infection (fever, increasing redness, drainage) |
| Follow weight-bearing restrictions per graft type | Sleep with a pillow under the knee (promotes flexion contracture) |
| Start patellar mobilizations immediately | Rush ROM flexion at the expense of extension |

## Phase 2: Early Rehabilitation (Weeks 2–6)

### Goals

- Maintain full extension (0°)
- Progress flexion toward **120° by week 4**, **135° by week 6**
- Restore normal gait pattern (wean off crutches per surgeon guidelines)
- Continue reducing swelling
- Progress quadriceps and hamstring strengthening

### Key Exercises — Phase 2

**Strengthening (Closed Kinetic Chain)**
- Mini-squats progressing to deeper range
- Leg press (limited range initially)
- Step-ups (low step, progressing height)
- Calf raises (bilateral progressing to single-leg)
- Wall sits with increasing hold times

**Open Kinetic Chain — The Evidence**

The safety of open kinetic chain (OKC) exercises like leg extensions has been debated for years. The current evidence supports their use with appropriate range restrictions:

- **Fukuda et al.** introduced supervised OKC exercises at 4 weeks post-op, mechanically locked between 45°–90° of knee flexion until 12 weeks. No increase in graft laxity was found.
- **Heijne & Werner** also began OKC at 4 weeks, progressing to full range by 6 weeks. Results varied by graft type — patellar tendon grafts showed no increased laxity, while hamstring grafts showed mixed results.
- A 2024 systematic review in *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living* confirmed that OKC exercises are generally safe and can significantly improve quadriceps strength without increasing graft laxity.
- ACL graft strain is essentially 0% when quad contractions occur at 60°–90° of knee flexion, which is why early OKC protocols restrict to this range.

**Bottom line**: OKC leg extensions are safe from ~4 weeks in a restricted range (90°–45° flexion arc). Discuss graft-specific timing with your surgeon, as hamstring grafts may warrant more caution.

**Neuromuscular and Balance**
- Single-leg stance progressions
- Balance board / BOSU ball work
- Weight shifting in multiple planes
- Proprioceptive training (perturbation exercises)

**Range of Motion**
- Stationary bike (when 110°+ flexion achieved) — excellent for ROM and low-impact conditioning
- Heel slides with active assist
- Prone knee flexion

### Do's and Don'ts — Phase 2

| DO | DON'T |
|-----|-------|
| Achieve full extension and maintain it every session | Begin running or impact activities |
| Introduce OKC quad work in a safe range (90°–45°) | Perform open chain exercises in full extension range yet |
| Begin eccentric quad work (evidence supports from ~3 weeks for CKC) | Ignore persistent swelling — it inhibits the quads |
| Ride stationary bike once ROM allows | Perform resisted hamstring curls aggressively if hamstring graft |
| Monitor for patellar tendinitis from overloading | Rush to single-leg exercises before adequate strength base |

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 6–12)

### Goals

- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Significant quad and hamstring strength gains
- Progress balance and proprioceptive challenges
- Begin cardiovascular conditioning
- Normalize gait on all surfaces including stairs

### Key Exercises — Phase 3

**Strengthening**
- Squats progressing to deeper range and added load
- Romanian deadlifts / single-leg deadlifts
- Leg press through increasing range
- OKC leg extensions — progress to fuller range (per surgeon guidance)
- Hamstring curls (prone and seated)
- Hip abduction / adduction with resistance
- Calf raises (single-leg)
- Core strengthening (planks, pallof press, dead bugs)

**Neuromuscular Training**
- Single-leg balance on unstable surfaces
- Lateral band walks
- Step-downs with controlled knee alignment (watch for valgus collapse)
- Perturbation training with increasing challenge

**Cardiovascular**
- Stationary bike with increasing resistance
- Elliptical trainer
- Swimming (once incisions healed and cleared)
- Pool walking / aqua jogging

### Do's and Don'ts — Phase 3

| DO | DON'T |
|-----|-------|
| Focus heavily on quadriceps strength — it's the #1 modifiable predictor of outcomes | Begin running before adequate quad strength and zero effusion |
| Train hip and core — they control knee alignment during dynamic tasks | Perform plyometrics or jumping yet |
| Monitor limb symmetry index for quad strength | Neglect hamstring-to-quad ratio (target H:Q ≥ 66%) |
| Begin controlled eccentric strengthening | Return to sport-specific drills |
| Address any movement quality deficits (knee valgus during squats/step-downs) | Ignore pain or swelling increases after sessions |

## Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening and Early Sport-Specific Training (Months 3–6)

### What the Research Says

Biological graft healing is a critical consideration. Graft remodeling ("ligamentization") takes 6–12+ months. The graft is at its weakest structural point between 6–12 weeks post-op during the avascular necrosis phase, then gradually strengthens but does not reach full maturation for well over a year.

### Goals

- Quad strength limb symmetry index (LSI) approaching ≥80%
- Begin running program (typically around month 4, criterion-based)
- Introduce plyometric foundations
- Develop sport-specific movement patterns
- Build cardiovascular endurance toward sport demands

### Running Readiness Criteria

Before starting a return-to-run program, the following criteria should be met:

- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Zero or trace effusion
- Quad strength LSI ≥70% (some protocols require ≥80%)
- Normal gait pattern without compensations
- Good single-leg balance (≥30 seconds)
- Ability to perform pain-free single-leg squats with good alignment

### Key Exercises — Phase 4

**Strength (Heavy Loading)**
- Barbell squats, split squats, Bulgarian split squats
- Deadlifts and single-leg Romanian deadlifts
- Leg press (full range, heavy)
- Single-leg leg press
- Nordic hamstring curls (eccentric hamstring strengthening — shown to reduce hamstring injuries)
- Hip thrust progressions

**Plyometric Foundations**
- Double-leg box jumps (focus on soft, controlled landing)
- Squat jumps (double-leg)
- Forward/backward hops (double-leg first)
- Drop landings from low box — emphasize knee-over-toe alignment, avoid valgus

**Running Progression**
- Walk/jog intervals on flat, even surface
- Gradual increase in jogging duration
- Progress to continuous jogging
- Forward running only initially (no cutting, pivoting, or lateral movement)

**Neuromuscular and Landing Mechanics**

This is where the work of **Hewett, Myer, and colleagues** becomes especially relevant. Their research established that knee abduction moment (valgus loading) during landing is a strong predictor of ACL injury. Training should specifically target:

- Landing mechanics: soft landings, knees tracking over toes, avoiding valgus collapse
- Hip and core control during dynamic tasks
- Single-leg landing control
- Deceleration mechanics

### Do's and Don'ts — Phase 4

| DO | DON'T |
|-----|-------|
| Use criterion-based (not time-based) milestones for running clearance | Start running based on a calendar date alone |
| Progress plyometrics from double-leg to single-leg gradually | Perform cutting, pivoting, or change-of-direction drills yet |
| Film yourself landing and squatting to check mechanics | Ignore asymmetries — if one leg compensates, address it |
| Continue heavy quad strengthening alongside plyometrics | Skip the boring strength work because running "feels fine" |
| Train landing mechanics explicitly — they don't improve on their own | Return to team training or scrimmages |

## Phase 5: Sport-Specific Training and Return-to-Sport Preparation (Months 6–9+)

### What the Research Says

The **Grindem et al. (2016)** Delaware-Oslo ACL Cohort Study is the most influential study on return-to-sport timing. Key findings:

- Reinjury risk was reduced by **51% for each month** return to sport was delayed, **up to 9 months** post-surgery
- After 9 months, no further risk reduction was observed from additional delay alone
- Athletes who passed both timing AND functional criteria had a reinjury rate of **5.6%**, compared to **38.2%** for those who failed
- Using these simple decision rules, it is possible to **reduce reinjury risk by 84%**

**Kyritsis et al. (2016)** studied 158 professional male athletes and found:

- 26 of 158 athletes (16.5% overall) sustained an ACL graft rupture
- Athletes who did NOT meet all 6 discharge criteria before returning to sport had a **4 times greater risk** of graft rupture (hazard ratio 4.1)
- Their test battery included: isokinetic quad/hamstring strength ratios (≥90% LSI at 60°/s, ≥90% LSI at 180°/s), single hop, triple hop, triple crossover hop (all ≥90% LSI), and running T-test (completed within 11 seconds)

### Goals

- Quad and hamstring strength LSI ≥90%
- Pass all hop tests at ≥90% LSI
- Demonstrate sport-specific competence (agility, cutting, pivoting)
- Achieve psychological readiness
- Pass comprehensive return-to-sport test battery

### Key Exercises — Phase 5

**Agility and Change of Direction**
- Lateral shuffles
- Carioca / grapevine
- Figure-8 running (large circles progressing to tighter)
- Cone drills and ladder drills
- Cutting progressions: planned cuts → reactive cuts → sport-specific scenarios
- Deceleration drills: sprinting to sudden stops

**Advanced Plyometrics**
- Single-leg box jumps
- Depth jumps with immediate rebound
- Lateral hops and bounds (single-leg)
- Sport-specific jumping patterns
- Reactive plyometrics (responding to visual/auditory cues)

**Sport-Specific Training**
- Gradual reintroduction of sport-specific skills (e.g., dribbling, kicking, catching while moving)
- Non-contact drills first, progressing to limited contact
- Practice simulations at increasing intensity

**The HIP-GREAT Program (Sugimoto et al., 2021)**

An 8-year prospective study compared traditional PT to the HIP-GREAT protocol (Hip-focused training with Graft Rupture Education and Avoidance Training) in young athletes (mean age ~17 years):

- Traditional PT group: **7.4%** graft rupture rate (10/136)
- HIP-GREAT group: **3.3%** graft rupture rate (5/153)
- While not statistically significant (p = .09), the trend toward reduced rupture was clinically meaningful
- The program emphasizes hip and core neuromuscular control combined with education about high-risk movement patterns

### Do's and Don'ts — Phase 5

| DO | DON'T |
|-----|-------|
| Progress from planned to reactive movements | Return to full competition before passing ALL criteria |
| Train sport-specific scenarios at graded intensity | Skip the psychological readiness component |
| Use video analysis of movement quality during sport drills | Assume that passing strength tests alone is enough |
| Maintain strength training throughout this phase | Stop doing rehab exercises once sport training begins |
| Implement a structured return-to-training plan with your coach | Go from zero sport exposure to full match play overnight |

## Phase 6: Return to Sport — The Decision

### Comprehensive Return-to-Sport Criteria

Based on the collective evidence, the recommended minimum criteria before full return to sport are:

**Timing**
- Minimum **9 months** post-surgery (Grindem et al., 2016)
- Some researchers and surgeons recommend 12 months for high-level pivoting sports

**Strength Testing**
- Isokinetic quadriceps strength LSI **≥90%** (at 60°/s and 180°/s)
- Isokinetic hamstring strength LSI **≥90%**
- Hamstring-to-quadriceps strength ratio **≥66%**

**Hop Testing (all ≥90% LSI)**
- Single hop for distance
- Triple hop for distance
- Crossover hop for distance
- 6-meter timed hop

**Functional Testing**
- Running T-test or equivalent agility test (Kyritsis: within 11 seconds)
- Quality of movement assessment during cutting, landing, and pivoting (no dynamic valgus)

**Patient-Reported Outcomes**
- KOOS-Sports/Recreation subscale **≥90%**
- KOS-ADLS **≥90%**

**Psychological Readiness**
- ACL-RSI score **≥80%** (Ardern et al., 2014)

The ACL-RSI scale, developed to measure psychological readiness, evaluates three domains: emotions, confidence in performance, and risk appraisal. Research consistently shows that fear of reinjury is the number one barrier to returning to sport (cited by ~44% of athletes), followed by lack of confidence in performance (~13%). Athletes who do not feel psychologically ready have significantly worse outcomes.

**Clinical Assessment**
- Zero or trace effusion
- Full range of motion
- No pain during sport-specific activities

### The Workload Transition

Even after passing all tests, the transition back to full sport must be managed. Research on acute-to-chronic workload ratios (ACWR) shows that ratios exceeding 2.0 increase injury risk. Since total workload drops dramatically after ACL injury, a **stepwise, progressive increase** in training volume and intensity is critical after clearance. Suggested progression:

1. **Weeks 1–2**: Modified training only, ~50% of team volume
2. **Weeks 3–4**: Increased volume, ~75%, introduce some contact
3. **Weeks 5–6**: Near-full training volume, full contact
4. **Weeks 7+**: Full unrestricted return to competition

## Key Risk Factors for Re-Tear

Based on a 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Sports Medicine*, the following factors significantly increase graft rupture risk:

| Risk Factor | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Return to sport before 9 months** | 51% higher risk per month earlier (Grindem 2016) |
| **Failing return-to-sport criteria** | 4x higher graft rupture risk; 16.5% overall rupture rate in study (Kyritsis 2016) |
| **Young age** | Athletes under 20 have the highest re-tear rates |
| **Allograft use** | Odds ratio of 5.56 for graft rupture compared to autograft |
| **Small graft size** | Odds ratio 2.2 for rupture within 4 years |
| **Quadriceps weakness** | Persistent LSI <90% at return to sport |
| **Dynamic knee valgus** | High knee abduction moment during landing (Hewett et al.) |
| **Low psychological readiness** | ACL-RSI <56 associated with not returning to sport |
| **Female sex (for second injury)** | Second ACL injury incidence in young females is 16x higher than primary ACL incidence |

## Summary: The Non-Negotiables

Based on the totality of the evidence, these are the practices most strongly associated with reduced re-tear rates:

1. **Do prehab**: 4–6 weeks of structured prehabilitation before surgery improves outcomes up to 10 years later (Zakharia et al., 2025).

2. **Prioritize quadriceps strength**: It is the single most important modifiable factor in recovery. Target ≥90% limb symmetry before return to sport.

3. **Use criterion-based progression**: Do not advance phases based on calendar dates alone. Meet objective benchmarks at every stage.

4. **Wait at least 9 months**: Each month of delay up to 9 months reduces reinjury risk by 51% (Grindem et al., 2016).

5. **Pass a comprehensive return-to-sport test battery**: Strength, hop tests, agility, patient-reported outcomes, AND psychological readiness. Passing all criteria is associated with 84% lower reinjury risk.

6. **Train landing mechanics and neuromuscular control**: Knee valgus during landing is a strong predictor of ACL injury and reinjury (Hewett & Myer). This doesn't fix itself — it requires targeted training.

7. **Address psychological readiness**: Fear of reinjury is the top barrier to return. Use the ACL-RSI scale and consider working with a sports psychologist if scores are low.

8. **Manage the return-to-sport workload transition**: Don't go from rehab to full competition overnight. Progressively ramp training volume over 6+ weeks.
